The goal of this task is to annotate the pre- and post-conditions of certain actions of interest in the manual.
We hereby define the essential terminologies:

  • Actionable: a phrase of which a person can really execute or take actions in the real world.

    Example Step: Prepare the line. The length and width of line you need varies based on your trimmer. If you purchase the wrong width of line, the trimmer will not work correctly, so don’t waste your money by simply guessing at the hardware store. If you are not sure what size line your trimmer uses, check online — the manufacturer’s website often has instructions, and if not the customer service department should be able to help you...

    • In this example, both "Prepare the line" and "purchase the wrong width of line" are valid actionables as you can really follow and execute them in the real world.
    • In contrast, "the manufacturer’s website often has instructions" is NOT an actionable as a person is not the main actor (website is) to perform the has action.
    • Note that we only show two actionables in this step for brevity.
    • You are required to find ALL the actionables of each step during the annotation.
    • Negation of an action can be a valid actionable if the instruction texts mention its cause or outcomes, e.g. in a recipe not heating the pan or fail to heat the pan are valid actionables if the outcome chicken will not be well-cooked is mentioned.

  • Pre-condition: The prerequisites to be met for an actionable to be executable, which can be a status, a condition, and/or another prior actionable.

    Example Step 1: Prepare the line. The length and width of line you need varies based on your trimmer. If you purchase the wrong width of line, the trimmer will not work correctly, so don’t waste your money by simply guessing at the hardware store. If you are not sure what size line your trimmer uses, check online — the manufacturer’s website often has instructions, and if not the customer service department should be able to help you...

    Example Step 2: Warm a pan with olive oil over medium heat. 2 tbsp. of olive oil should be enough if it can thinly coat the bottom of the pan. Wait until the oil is sizzling, which should take about 1-2 minutes.Place the chicken in the pan. The boiling oil will make the surface of the chicken legs golden and crispy. You can stop frying when the chicken is darkened.

    • The pre-condition can be describing a status, e.g. in Example 1, one needs to be "not sure what size line your trimmer uses" before taking the action "check online", which also requires the website to have the instructions ready as a pre-condition. Also, in Example 2, one needs to wait until the "oil is sizzling" before frying the chicken, this phrase is another required pre-condition to the actionable "Place the chicken in the pan"
    • The pre-condition can be an actionable, e.g. in Example 2, prior to "Place the chicken in the pan", one needs to first "Warm a pan with olive oil over medium heat.", and hence the latter is the pre-condition to the former.
    • Please annotate ALL the possible pre-conditions to each valid actionable.

  • Post-condition: The outcome (effect) caused by the execution of an actionable.

    Example Step: Prepare the line. The length and width of line you need varies based on your trimmer. If you purchase the wrong width of line, the trimmer will not work correctly, so don’t waste your money by simply guessing at the hardware store. If you are not sure what size line your trimmer uses, check online — the manufacturer’s website often has instructions, and if not the customer service department should be able to help you...

    • "the trimmer will not work correctly" is the result caused by the actionable "purchase the wrong width of line" and hence the former is the post-condition to the latter.
    • In this example, "simply guessing at the hardware store." will lead to the result that you will "waste your money", and hence the latter is the post-condition to the former.
    • Note that a post-condition can possibly be regarded as the pre-condition for a subsequent actionable.
    • Please again annotate ALL the possible post-conditions to each valid actionable.

Below is the summarized step-by-step annotation flow to recap the quick-start video:

As can be seen we have pre-highlighted a few statements in grey where each of them is clickable.
These statements are automatically pre-selected using certain algorithms, which are supposed to
cover as much potential actionables and pre-/post-conditions as possible, but they may not be that accurate!

The annotation flow:
  1. Annotate an Actionable: Select a clickable pre-highlighted text segment if it is a valid actionable. You can also actively highlight a text segment using the mouse cursor should you feel the pre-highlighted texts are not accurate enough (e.g. containing redundant information). Once a text segment is clicked or highlighted, the color will change to yellow and a corresponding text block will pop out on the right panel.
    • You can click on the X button on the text block if you wish to remove the clicked or highlighted texts.
    • Please annotate actionable text blocks even if no valid pre- and post-conditions exist in the instruction text, simply leave these actionables unconnected.

  2. Annotate the Pre-conditions: Click or highlight ALL the text segments that are suitable pre-conditions to the actionable found in (1). Then, on the right panel simply right-click on a pre-condition text block and start to drag an (directed) edge to connect to the actionable text block, and then release the drag.
    • Note that a pre-condition of an actionable can be another actionable or a statement describing a status which needs to be met prior to executing the actionable.
    • The direction of the edge implies the temporal order of the events, i.e. event A ⟼ event B indicates that event A happens before event B.

  3. Label the Edge: By right-clicking on the edge, please select from the drop-down menu the proper tag (pre-condition) to label the relations.

  4. Annotate the Post-conditions: Similar to (2), find ALL suitable post-conditions to the actionable found in (1). Simply right-click on the actionable text block and start to drag an (directed) edge to connect to the post-condition text block.
    • A post-condition of an actionable is usually a statement describing a status which is caused after (by) executing the actionable.

  5. Label the Edge: Similar to (3), please select from the drop-down menu the proper tag (post-condition) to label the relations.

  6. Repeat from (1) to (5) until you cannot find anymore valid relations to be annotated among your selected text blocks.
Notes:
  • We highly recommend you to read the entire manual at least once before starting the annotation.
  • Please annotate ALL the valid actionables regardless of whether their pre/post-conditions exist (in the text) or not.
    • For some status descriptions, however, they should only be annotated if they are the pre- or post-conditions of some actionables.
  • When annotating actionables, do not feel hesitated to highlight as much as possible if all the presented information is essential and the segment is self-contained, e.g.
    • You should annotate Look for a strong sponge that has a soft side and a abrasive side that won't scratch your bottle rather than Look for a strong sponge.

The FAQ is meant to clarify some potential uncertainties you may encounter when doing this task.
You do not need to read it fully now, but you may find it useful and come back to check the details after you start the task.
We list a few cases one may feel unsure about with some examples and clarifications
Content:
  1. Make sure statement
  2. If statement
  3. Negation
  4. If A is the pre-condition of B, does that mean B is the post-condition of A?
  5. Conjunctions: before, after, and until
  6. Passive actionable
  7. When the title of the step and a step set forth after the title both refer to the exact same action
Cases:
  1. Make sure statement: The rule of thumb is, if the statement is determined to be a valid post-condition of an actionable, it should be labeled as a post-condition, otherwise, it should be an actionable, e.g.

    Examples:

    1. Fry the chicken, make sure it's cooked fully.
    2. Make sure you have the wire, and connect the wire.
    3. Make sure to knock on the door first, and enter.

    • In the first example, it's cooked fully is the post-condition of Fry the chicken.
    • In the second example, Make sure you have the wire is the pre-condition of connect the wire.
    • In the third one, Make sure to knock on the door first is just an actionable.

  2. If statement:

    Examples:

    1. If you cook the chicken leg wrong, you'll end up with something that is dry and rubbery.
    2. If you are not sure where to buy it, you can check it online.

    • First, end up with something that is dry and rubbery is the post-condition of cook the chicken leg wrong.
    • Second, not sure where to buy it is the pre-conditon of check it online.

  3. Negation:

    Examples:

    1. Don't waste your money by simply guessing at the hardware store.
    2. Avoid cotton cloths and towels, which could leave residue on your jewelry.
    3. If you're lighting a fire indoors, you probably won't need to worry about the flame going out.

    • First, waste your money is the post-condition of simply guessing at the hardware store.
    • Sometimes, for negated action, you can annotate the phrase but ignore the verb/predicate if you find valid condition-actionable relation. In the second example, you can ignore the verb "Avoid". The real actionable can be something like use cotton cloths and towels (but of course use does not exist in the sentence) which leads to post-condition leave residue on your jewelry.
    • Negation words and phrases can also be included in an actionable. For example, lighting a fire indoors means that you won't need to worry about the flame going out.

  4. A post-condition should refer to the status or the results after applying/executing an actionable, for example:

    Examples:

    1. Add the ramen.
    2. Boil the noodles for 3 minutes.

    • 1 is 2's pre-condition indeed, but 2 is NOT 1's post-condition. A pre-condition enables an action being executable, but this action is not necessarily the result after applying the action.
    • In this case, the real post-conditions of 1 should be, "ramen in the water", or "water covers ramen", and the post-condition for 2 can be "ramen is softened", etc.

  5. Conjunctions: before, after, and until: When you see conjunctions such as before, after, and until, it is very likely that there are some pre-/post-conditions. Some examples:

    Examples:

    1. Soak the sponge in the solution until it is drenched.
    2. Dry the brush and holder before storing them
    3. After washing the vegitables, put them in the pot.

    • "Do X until Y happens" implies that Y is the consequence of X, hence a post-condition.
    • "Do X before doing Y can imply X is a pre-condition that needs to happen for Y to be executed. Although in this example you indeed need to dry the brush before you can store them, be aware that temporal order does not always imply pre-conditions unless the events are dependent. E.g. “cut carrots before cutting cucumber”, in reality, cutting cucumber does not really depend on if you cut the carrots or not.
    • Similar to the before case, "After doing X, do Y" can imply X is a pre-condition to Y.

  6. Passive actionables: A phrase that describes scenarios that a person passively receives or indirectly performs an action can be considered as a valid actionable.

    Examples:

    1. If your application is approved, you'll get a letter in the mail.

    • In the first example, the phrase you'll get a letter in the mail at first glance is not an executable statement, however, someone else has to send that letter in order for a person to get a letter.
    • Moreoever, we can clearly see a pre-condition relation as the application being approved is required for that letter to be sent.
    • As this type of knowledge is useful and important as well, we ask you to annotate these relations if you recognize their presence in the instruction texts.

  7. When the title of the step and a step set forth after the title both refer to the exact same action.

    Examples:

    1. Step 1: Heat the butter until it melts. Place the bowl in the microwave. Heat the butter on high until it is all melted.
    2. Step 2: Let the butter sit. Leave the butter alone in the microwave for 3 to 5 minutes.
    3. Step 3: Use a spoon to skim off the top.

    • This example can be a bit confusing because both Step 1 and Step 2 follow the structure of "title of a step" + "detailed steps referring to the same action".
    • Let's first start with relations within Step 1, Place the bowl in the microwave must happen before Heat the butter on high so we can see a pre-condition relationship here.
    • Between Step 1 and Step 2, while Heat the butter is a pre-condition of Let the butter sit, Heat the butter on high refers to the same action so it can also be a precondition of Let the butter sit.
    • Similarly, Heat the butter on high is the pre-condition of both Let the butter sit and Leave the butter alone in the microwave for 3 to 5 minutes.
    • Lastly, between Step 2 and Step 3, as Leave the butter alone in the microwave for 3 to 5 minutes is a detailed version of Let the butter sit, we can say that they describe the same action so they are all pre-conditions of Use a spoon the skim off the top.
    • Please find as many relations as you can in an article.
